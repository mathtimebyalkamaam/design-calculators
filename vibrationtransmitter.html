Engineering Reference & Vibration Analysis Bible

Industrial Vibration Monitoring: The What, Why, Which, Where & How

1. WHAT is Vibration Monitoring and how does this tool calculate values?

Vibration monitoring is the process of measuring casing or shaft movement to identify mechanical anomalies like unbalance, misalignment, rotor looseness, or bearing wear before they lead to catastrophic equipment failure. This tool performs standardized mechanical conversions using calculus-based relationships. Since vibration speed is the derivative of displacement and the integral of acceleration, we convert between units at a specific operating speed frequency ($f$) using the angular velocity $\omega = 2\pi f$. For instance, metric conversions are calculated as:

$$ V_{rms} = \frac{A_{rms} \times 9806.65}{2\pi f} \text{ [g RMS to mm/s RMS]} $$ $$ V_{rms} = D_{rms} \times 2\pi f \times 10^{-3} \text{ [µm RMS to mm/s RMS]} $$

2. WHY is the Crest Factor ($CF$) vital for bearing failure detection?

The Crest Factor ($CF = \text{Peak} / \text{RMS}$) indicates the "spikiness" of a vibration waveform. Pure sinusoidal motion has a Crest Factor of $1.414$. However, early rolling element bearing failures (e.g. spalling, cracking) produce high-amplitude, short-duration impact spikes. These spikes raise the peak value significantly while having negligible effect on the overall RMS energy, pushing the Crest Factor to $3.0$, $5.0$, or higher. If a default $1.414$ factor is used for a spiky signal, the calculated RMS velocity will be severely underestimated, masking bearing defects. This analyzer allows you to configure custom Crest Factors to ensure accurate RMS velocity zoning from peak sensor readings.

3. WHICH parameters and standards apply to my machinery installation?

Selecting the right vibration parameter and standard is critical to avoid false alarms or structural dropouts:
• ISO 20816-3 (Superseding ISO 10816-3): Governing industrial machines with rated power $>15\text{ kW}$ and speed range $120$ to $15000\text{ RPM}$. It splits machines into Group 1 (large machines $>300\text{ kW}$) and Group 2 (medium machines $15$ to $300\text{ kW}$).
• ISO 20816-7: Applicable strictly to rotodynamic pumps $>15\text{ kW}$. Category I represents high-integrity critical pumps, and Category II represents general service pumps.
• API 610 / API 670: API 610 applies to centrifugal pump factory testing overall bearing housing casing limits. API 670 governs machinery protection casing alerts ($5.0\text{ mm/s RMS}$) and danger shutdown trips ($10.0\text{ mm/s RMS}$).
• IS 14881: The Bureau of Indian Standards (BIS) national standard for mechanical vibration evaluation of industrial machinery on non-rotating parts, which mirrors the ISO 10816 classification matrix.

4. WHERE should casing transmitters be mounted for maximum accuracy?

Transmitters must be placed directly on the bearing housing or casing to minimize signal loss. Mount casing sensors in three orthogonal axes:
1. Radial Horizontal: Sensed perpendicular to the shaft. Best for detecting unbalance and structural looseness.
2. Radial Vertical: Sensed perpendicular to the shaft. Catches structural stiffness issues, frame weakness, and foundation cracking.
3. Axial: Sensed parallel to the shaft center line. Best for detecting thrust bearing failure, misaligned couplings, and bent shafts.

5. HOW do I size the 4-20mA current loop to prevent dropout?

In current loops, the transmitter acts as a variable current regulator. The terminal headroom voltage ($V_{tx}$) must be $\ge 12.0\text{ VDC}$ at the maximum loop current of $20\text{ mA}$ to maintain regulation loop stability. Copper wire resistance increases with operating temperatures, so wire loop resistance at temperature ($T$) is adjusted using the copper temperature coefficient $\alpha = 0.00393/\text{°C}$: $$ R_{cable} = R_{base} \times [1 + 0.00393 \times (T_{ambient} - 20)] \times 2 \times \frac{\text{Distance}}{1000} $$ If total loop resistance ($R_{cable} + R_{receiver}$) is too high, the voltage drop will reduce terminal voltage below $12.0\text{ VDC}$, causing the signal to clip or drop out when vibration peaks. This analyzer performs these checks in real-time, verifying conductor wire gauges, temperatures, and loop supply stability.

1. The Trinity: Acceleration, Velocity, and Displacement

Vibration can be measured in three different physical parameters. Choosing the right one depends on the frequency of interest and the type of defect. Understanding the mathematical relationship between these units ($D \rightarrow V \rightarrow A$) via integration and differentiation is fundamental to vibration analysis.

  • Displacement ($\mu m$, mils): Measures the total distance the shaft moves. It is heavily weighted toward Low Frequencies ($< 10$ Hz). It is the standard for monitoring Sleeve Bearings (Journal Bearings) using Eddy Current Probes (Proximity Probes), where the critical concern is the shaft physical movement relative to the babbitt lining (oil film clearance). It typically ignores high-frequency bearing noise because a very high frequency vibration often has a microscopic displacement amplitude.
  • Velocity (mm/s, ips): Measures the speed of the vibration. It is the ISO standard because it represents the Energy (Destructive Power) of the vibration. Energy is proportional to velocity squared ($E \propto v^2$). It is relatively flat across the frequency spectrum (10 Hz - 1000 Hz), making it the ideal "General Health" indicator for detecting unbalance, misalignment, and looseness in general rotating machinery.
  • Acceleration ($g$, m/s²): Measures the force ($F=ma$). It is heavily weighted toward High Frequencies ($> 1 kHz$). It is the primary tool for detecting Rolling Element Bearing faults and Gear Mesh issues. A tiny pit in a bearing race generates a sharp impact (high force/acceleration) but negligible movement (displacement). Therefore, acceleration is the earliest warning indicator for bearing failure.

The Mathematical Link: Physics dictates that these are related by integration/differentiation. If you know the speed frequency ($f$) and the Crest Factor ($CF$), you can convert them. Note that as frequency ($f$) increases, Displacement drops rapidly, while Acceleration rises.

$$ D_{rms} = \frac{D_{pk}}{CF} = \frac{D_{p-p}}{2 \times CF} \\ $$ $$ V_{rms} = D_{rms} \times 2\pi f \times 10^{-3} \text{ (Metric: }\mu\text{m to mm/s)} \\ $$ $$ V_{rms} = \frac{A_{rms} \times 9806.65}{2\pi f} \text{ (Metric: g to mm/s)} $$

2. Decoding ISO 20816-3 / 10816-3 Standards

ISO 20816-3 (which supersedes ISO 10816-3) is the global standard for evaluating vibration severity on non-rotating parts (bearing housings). It categorizes severity not just by the vibration number, but by the machine size, rated power, and foundation stiffness. A vibration of 5 mm/s might be acceptable for a giant fan on flexible springs but catastrophic for a rigid pump.

The 4 Severity Zones:

  • Zone A (Green): Vibration of newly commissioned machines. Ideal operating state.
  • Zone B (Yellow-Green): Acceptable for unrestricted long-term operation. Normal aging limits.
  • Zone C (Orange): Unsatisfactory. The machine can operate for a limited time to reach the next maintenance window, but remedial action is required. This represents the "Alarm" or "Alert" level in a DCS.
  • Zone D (Red): Unacceptable. Vibration is sufficient to cause structural fatigue damage. Immediate trip/shutdown is highly recommended to prevent catastrophic failure.

Rigid vs. Flexible Foundations: This is a critical distinction often missed. A machine on a massive concrete block (Rigid) has lower vibration tolerance limits because the foundation doesn't move with the machine; high vibration measurements indicate high internal stress on the bearings and housing. A machine on a flexible steel skid (Flexible) is allowed higher vibration numbers because the whole skid moves with the machine, absorbing some of the energy and reducing the internal stress on the bearings. **Always classify your foundation correctly to avoid false alarms.**

3. The 4-20mA Current Loop & Sizing Calculations

Vibration transmitters convert the complex dynamic vibration waveform into a simple DC current proportional to the RMS velocity. This allows a PLC or DCS to monitor health without needing expensive spectrum analyzers or high-speed DAQ cards.

Scaling Calculation: The transmitter has a "Full Scale" range (e.g., 25 mm/s). The 4-20mA output maps linearly to this range.

$$ I_{out} = 4\text{ mA} + \left( \frac{V_{rms}}{\text{Full Scale Range}} \right) \times 16\text{ mA} $$

Electrical Loop Sizing and Symmetrical Diagnostics: In addition to transmitter scaling, you must calculate the loop voltage headroom. The voltage at the transmitter terminals ($V_{tx}$) must exceed its minimum operating voltage ($V_{min}$, usually $12\text{ VDC}$) at the maximum loop current of $20\text{ mA}$:

$$ V_{tx\_min} = V_{supply} - I_{max} \times (R_{rx} + R_{cable}) \ge 12.0\text{ VDC} $$ $$ R_{cable\_max} = \frac{V_{supply} - 12.0}{0.020} - R_{rx} $$

If $V_{tx\_min} < 12.0\text{ VDC}$, the loop will drop out or behave erratically when vibration levels increase, leading to false trips or loss of signal integrity.

4. RMS vs. Peak vs. Peak-to-Peak

Confusion between these units is the #1 cause of alarm configuration errors in industrial vibration monitoring.

  • RMS (Root Mean Square): Represents the "Area under the curve" or power content. ISO standards use RMS velocity. Most 4-20mA transmitters output RMS. It is a statistical average that correlates well with destructive energy.
  • Peak (0-pk): The maximum distance from zero to the top of the wave. Often used for Acceleration to catch impulsive impacts. For a pure sine wave, $Peak = RMS \times 1.414$. However, for a spiky waveform (like a bad bearing), Peak can be 5x or 10x the RMS value (High Crest Factor).
  • Peak-to-Peak (pk-pp): The total distance from the bottom trough to the top crest. Standard for Displacement (Orbit analysis) on sleeve bearings. $Pk-Pk = Peak \times 2$.

Warning: If your DCS expects RMS but you program the alarm limits using Peak values from a chart, you will alarm too late (under-sensitive). If you use Peak values for RMS limits, you will false alarm constantly (over-sensitive). This calculator handles the conversions automatically utilizing the user's Crest Factor.

5. Sensor Mounting Best Practices

The method used to mount the sensor dictates the frequency response range.

  • Stud Mount (Drilled & Tapped): The gold standard. Direct mechanical transfer allows reading frequencies up to 10-15 kHz. Essential for high-speed gearboxes.
  • Adhesive/Glue Mount: Good for up to 5-8 kHz if done correctly with a stiff epoxy.
  • Magnet Mount: Convenient for walk-around routes but acts like a low-pass filter. Typically loses accuracy above 2 kHz. Fine for unbalance (1X), bad for early bearing faults.
  • Hand-Held Probe (Stinger): The worst method. Severely dampens signal. Only useful for very low frequency (< 500 Hz) measurements.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

1. What is the difference between vibration velocity, acceleration, and displacement?

Vibration can be measured in three physical parameters. Displacement (measured in mils or microns pk-pk) represents the physical travel distance of motion, critical for low frequency (<10 Hz) and sleeve journal bearings. Velocity (measured in mm/s or ips RMS) represents the speed of vibration and directly correlates to mechanical fatigue energy in the mid-frequency range (10-1000 Hz). Acceleration (measured in g or m/s² RMS/Peak) measures structural force and is the most sensitive metric for high-frequency impacts (>1000 Hz) such as gear tooth pitting and rolling element bearing defects.

2. How does the Crest Factor affect vibration severity conversions?

The Crest Factor (CF) is the ratio of the peak value to the RMS value of a waveform. In pure sinusoidal waves (like simple unbalance), the Crest Factor is exactly $\sqrt{2} \approx 1.414$. However, real-world machines with rolling element bearing wear generate short, high-energy impact spikes, resulting in crest factors of 3.0 to 5.0+. Adjusting the Crest Factor allows accurate conversion of peak metrics to RMS (and vice versa) to prevent false alerts or undetected bearing housing failures.

3. What is the difference between rigid and flexible foundations under ISO 20816-3?

Under ISO 20816-3, a machine's foundation is classified as Rigid if its natural frequencies are significantly higher than the machine's operating speed (typical for heavy concrete foundations). A foundation is classified as Flexible if its natural frequencies are near or below the operating speed (typical for steel skids or frame supports). Flexible foundations are permitted higher overall vibration limits because the foundation components absorb some of the vibration energy, reducing the direct stress applied to the machine's bearing housings.

4. How do I configure a 4-20mA loop for a vibration transmitter?

A standard 4-20mA current loop is wired in series, consisting of a 24VDC loop power supply, the field vibration transmitter, and a receiver load resistor (typically 250 ohms at the DCS or PLC analog input module). 4mA corresponds to 0 mm/s (no vibration), and 20mA corresponds to the full-scale range (e.g. 25 mm/s RMS). The 250-ohm resistor converts the 4-20mA current to a 1-5V analog voltage input for digital conversion inside the control system.

5. How is the maximum cable length determined for a 4-20mA loop?

The maximum cable run is limited by the loop supply voltage ($V_s$), the receiver load ($R_{rx}$), and the transmitter's minimum operating voltage ($V_{min}$, usually $12\text{ VDC}$). The maximum allowable round-trip cable resistance is $R_{cable} = \frac{V_s - V_{min}}{0.020} - R_{rx}$. For a 24V supply and 250-ohm load, $R_{cable\_max} = \frac{24 - 12}{0.020} - 250 = 350\ \Omega$. For standard AWG 18 cable (approx $21\ \Omega/\text{km}$), this corresponds to a maximum one-way distance of $\approx 8.3\text{ km}$ ($27,000\text{ feet}$).

6. What do the ISO 20816-3 severity zones (A, B, C, D) represent?

The severity zones are defined as follows:
• Zone A (Green): Vibration levels typical of newly commissioned machinery.
• Zone B (Yellow-Green): Vibration levels acceptable for unrestricted long-term operation.
• Zone C (Orange): Vibration levels unsatisfactory for continuous operation. The machine can operate temporarily but maintenance should be planned (DCS Alarm).
• Zone D (Red): Unacceptable vibration levels that can cause structural damage or catastrophic fatigue failure. Immediate shutdown recommended (DCS Trip).

7. What is API 670 and what are its casing vibration alarm recommendations?

API 670 is the American Petroleum Institute standard for Machinery Protection Systems. It defines the hardware and configuration criteria for vibration sensors, transmitters, and monitors. For casing vibration monitoring, API 670 recommends a standard Alert (Alarm) limit of 5.0 mm/s RMS (0.2 ips RMS) and a Danger (Trip) limit of 10.0 mm/s RMS (0.4 ips RMS) to protect critical machinery from catastrophic failure.

8. What are the bearing housing vibration limits under API 610?

API 610 specifies factory acceptance test limits for centrifugal pumps in petroleum and gas services. For bearing housing vibration measured on the test stand at rated operating speed, the limit is 3.0 mm/s RMS (0.12 ips RMS) for pumps on rigid test supports and 4.5 mm/s RMS (0.18 ips RMS) for pumps on flexible test supports, ensuring excellent structural integrity before dispatch.

9. How do I convert shaft displacement (mils pk-pk) to velocity (mm/s RMS)?

To convert displacement to velocity:
1. Convert mils peak-to-peak displacement to microns: $D_{\mu m} = D_{mils} \times 25.4$.
2. Convert peak-to-peak to RMS using the Crest Factor ($CF$): $D_{rms} = \frac{D_{\mu m}}{2 \times CF}$.
3. Differentiate to Velocity RMS: $V_{rms} = \frac{D_{rms} \times 2\pi f}{1000}$.
For example, at 50 Hz speed, 4 mils pk-pk displacement translates to $11.1\text{ mm/s RMS}$ using a crest factor of 1.414.

10. Why is RMS velocity the standard unit for general machinery vibration health?

RMS velocity represents the root-mean-square of the vibration velocity, which is directly proportional to the kinetic energy and mechanical fatigue inside the machine. Because mechanical energy (and thus fatigue stress on bearings and casing structures) is proportional to velocity squared, RMS velocity provides a consistent, mathematically sound indicator of potential machine damage across a broad frequency band (10 to 1000 Hz).
